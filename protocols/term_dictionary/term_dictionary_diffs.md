### why this matters

standard pali and sanskrit dictionaries are **synchronic** — they present a term's meanings as a flat list, as though all senses coexisted simultaneously. the term dictionary is **diachronic** — it insists that meanings have temporal strata, and that the sequence matters. nearly every disagreement reduces to one pattern: **the standard reference flattens what our dictionary stratifies**. this is not a minor lexicographic quibble. the book's entire argument (temporal flattening thesis, stance drift, method-hardening) depends on showing that terms mean different things in different periods, and that collapsing those differences obscures what the buddha actually did.

## 4. scholarly implications — what these disagreements collectively argue

### the temporal flattening thesis

the 34 disagreements are not random. they express a single structural pattern: **standard dictionaries collapse temporal strata into synchronic synonym lists, erasing the sequence that constitutes the semantic drift**.

this pattern operates at three levels:

**level 1: vedic-to-buddhist flattening**
terms like brahman, atman, deva, agni, indra, tapas, and yajna underwent radical semantic transformation between the rgveda and the pali canon. standard dictionaries record the endpoints but not the trajectory. when PTS says aggi = "fire," it has already completed the demotion that the term dictionary tracks (deva-priest → craving metaphor). the dictionary's silence is the drift's success.

**level 2: early-to-late buddhist flattening**
terms like anatta, sunna, vipassana, sati, jhana, samadhi, and kammatthana mean different things in the sutta period versus the commentarial period. PTS and wisdomlib present the later sense (mental factor, three characteristics, 40 meditation subjects) as though it were the original. this makes the method-hardening that ch3 documents invisible — because the dictionary has already performed the hardening.

**level 3: cross-tradition flattening**
terms like natthika/nastika, issara/isvara, and jiva mean different things in buddhist, brahmanical, and jain frameworks. MW's "atheist" for nastika and "soul" for jiva import brahmanical and western categories into buddhist context. this makes the "wrong question" that ch5 addresses look like a real question — because the dictionary has already framed it in theistic terms.

### what the diffs collectively demonstrate

1. **dictionaries are not neutral instruments** — they embed interpretive decisions, and those decisions tend toward later, more systematic readings.

2. **the drift the book tracks is confirmed by the reference works themselves** — PTS already systematizes where suttas are experiential, wisdomlib already presents commentary as original, MW already imports brahmanical categories into buddhist terms.

3. **the term dictionary is a scholarly intervention** — by insisting on temporal strata, it makes visible what flat dictionaries hide. each diff is a specific case where the temporal dimension matters for the book's argument.

4. **the 7 flagged [!] disagreements mark the book's strongest claims** — these are where the temporal stratification produces a reading that contradicts the standard reference, and where the book must explicitly present its evidence (grammatical form of anatta, qualifier "of self" in sunna, sutta frequency of vipassana, sutta meaning of kammatthana, metaphor difference between moksha and nibbana, nāstika/natthika framework-relativity, and the brahman trifurcation).
