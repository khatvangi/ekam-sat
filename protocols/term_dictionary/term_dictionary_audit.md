## 3. flagged items (standard reference disagreements)

### `anatta` (not-self / no-self / selflessness)
**chapter:** ch1 (anatta drift)

- **PTS:** anattā: not self, without a self
- **MW:** anātman: not self, not the soul

**disagreement:**

> PTS gives "not self, without a self" — preserving the adjective sense. MW gives "not self, not the soul" — 'soul' is MW's import (no pāli equivalent carries the Abrahamic substance-soul sense); reading is closer to the ontological interpretation. our dictionary insists: the PĀLI grammatical form (adjective, not noun) is evidence that the early teaching is 'not-self' (applied to aggregates), not 'no-self' (universal metaphysical claim). [!] FLAGGED — the adjective→noun shift in translations is itself the drift.

### `sunna` (empty / void / emptiness)
**chapter:** ch1 (anatta drift)

- **PTS:** suñña: empty, void
- **MW:** —

**disagreement:**

> PTS gives bare "empty, void" — missing the crucial qualifier 'of self' that defines the early usage. our dictionary insists: early suñña = 'empty of self' (SN 35.85), NOT 'empty' in the abstract. [!] FLAGGED — PTS's bare 'empty' already reflects the drift.

### `brahman` (the sacred / the absolute / the priestly)
**chapter:** ch2 (vedic baseline)

- **PTS:** brahma: the supreme being, a brahmin, holy, sacred
- **MW:** brahman: n. the one self-existent Spirit, the Absolute

**disagreement:**

> PTS CONFLATES neuter brahman (absolute) with masculine brahmā (deity). this conflation is itself evidence of drift — in pāli, the vedic/upanishadic distinction between neuter-absolute and masculine-deity has partially collapsed, making the buddhist strategic reinterpretation harder to see. our dictionary maintains the distinction because it matters for the stance index. [!] FLAGGED DISAGREEMENT WITH PTS.

### `samsara` (cycle of rebirth / transmigration)
**chapter:** ch2 (vedic baseline)

- **PTS:** saṃsāra: journeying, transmigration
- **MW:** saṃsāra: going about, wandering, transmigration

**disagreement:**

> CRITICAL: neither dictionary notes that saṃsāra is ABSENT from the ṛgveda. this absence is foundational evidence for the temporal stratification thesis. the concept emerges in the upanishadic period and is then inherited by both buddhism and jainism. [!] FLAGGED — standard references miss this.

### `moksha` (liberation / release)
**chapter:** ch2 (vedic baseline)

- **PTS:** nibbāna: going out, quenching, emancipation, the final goal
- **MW:** mokṣa: emancipation, liberation, release from worldly existence

**disagreement:**

> PTS lists "going out, quenching" first — preserving the fire metaphor. MW's "liberation, release" implies an agent being released. our dictionary insists the metaphor difference IS the philosophical difference: mokṣa assumes a self being freed; nibbāna does not. [!] FLAGGED — this lexical observation is a contribution.

### `vipassana` (insight / clear seeing / discernment)
**chapter:** ch3 (method drift)

- **PTS:** inward vision, intuition, introspection
- **MW:** vipaśyanā not listed (too buddhist-specific)

**disagreement:**

> [!] wisdomlib defines vipassanā as 'clear seeing into the three characteristics (tilakkhaṇa)' — this is the COMMENTARIAL definition, not the sutta one. the suttas never define vipassanā as seeing impermanence/suffering/not-self. that connection is made later.

### `kammatthana` (meditation subject / field of work / basis for practice)
**chapter:** ch3 (method drift)

- **PTS:** kammaṭṭhāna: working-Loss, occupation; in later usage: meditation subject
- **MW:** karmasthāna: not listed separately

**disagreement:**

> [!] wisdomlib gives ONLY the commentarial meaning ('meditation subject') without noting the sutta meaning ('field of enterprise'). PTS correctly notes 'in later usage' — the temporal strata matter. the drift from 'occupation' to 'meditation subject' is invisible in wisdomlib's flat definition.

### `nibbana` (extinction / unbinding / the going-out of the fires of craving)
**chapter:** ch4 (nibbana drift)

- **PTS:** nibbāna: going out, quenching, emancipation, the dying out of the three fires of rāga, dosa, moha
- **MW:** nirvāṇa: blowing out, extinction, cessation, complete happiness

**disagreement:**

> PTS preserves the fire metaphor ("going out, quenching") AND adds "emancipation" — blending the metaphor with a soteriological gloss. MW gives "blowing out, extinction" — accurate but then adds "complete happiness" which imports a positive definition the early texts avoid. our dictionary insists: the early texts use POINTING language (33 epithets, fire simile, release-chain), NOT defining language. the abhidhamma shift to "asaṅkhata dhamma" is where definition begins. [!] FLAGGED — both PTS and MW reflect post-abhidhamma consolidation, not the early multiplicity of epithets.

### `viraga` (dispassion / fading away / detachment)
**chapter:** ch4 (nibbana drift)

- **PTS:** virāga: dispassion, absence of desire, disgust
- **MW:** —

**disagreement:**

> PTS adds "disgust" — which is misleading. virāga is not aversion (dosa) but the FADING of passion. "disgust" implies an active emotional reaction; virāga implies the ABSENCE of reaction. [!] FLAGGED — PTS conflates virāga with paṭigha.

### `nibbida` (disenchantment / turning away / weariness with saṃsāra)
**chapter:** ch4 (nibbana drift)

- **PTS:** nibbidā: weariness, disgust, aversion
- **MW:** —

**disagreement:**

> PTS gives "weariness, disgust, aversion" — "disgust" and "aversion" are WRONG. nibbidā is not paṭigha (aversion) or dosa (hatred). it is the FADING of fascination, not the arising of rejection. [!] FLAGGED — PTS's "disgust" is a colonial mistranslation that makes the path sound emotionally negative rather than cognitively clarifying.

### `nirodha` (cessation / stopping / the third noble truth)
**chapter:** ch4 (nibbana drift)

- **PTS:** nirodha: cessation, destruction, annihilation; dying out
- **MW:** —

**disagreement:**

> PTS gives "cessation, destruction, annihilation" — "annihilation" is the colonial misreading again. nirodha is NOT annihilation (uccheda). the buddha explicitly rejects uccheda as a wrong view. nirodha is FUNCTIONAL cessation (craving stops, suffering stops), not ontological annihilation (something is destroyed). [!] FLAGGED — PTS's "annihilation" imports the uccheda-vāda (annihilationism) that the buddha condemns.

### `natthika` (nihilist / one who denies / nāstika)
**chapter:** ch5 (wrong question)

- **PTS:** natthika: nihilist, one who professes the doctrine of nihilism
- **MW:** nāstika: an atheist, an unbeliever, infidel

**disagreement:**

> MW defines nāstika as "atheist" — THIS IS THE PROBLEM. the buddhist natthika (one who denies karma) ≠ the brahmanical nāstika (one who denies vedic authority). MW's "atheist" is a Western category retrofitted onto a fundamentally different classificatory system. [!] FLAGGED — MW's translation is itself evidence of the confusion ch5 addresses.

### `vasavatti` (wielding power / exercising control)
**chapter:** ch5 (wrong question)

- **PTS:** vasavattī: exercising power, being in control
- **MW:** —

**disagreement:**

> PTS gives the positive sense. the DN 1 issaranimmāna context is not flagged.
