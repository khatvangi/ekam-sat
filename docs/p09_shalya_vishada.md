# The Viṣāda of Endgame

## Duryodhana in the Lake, and the Question of How a Kṣatriya Falls

---

### The Dimension

The Śalyaparva stages the final hours of the war. Śalya is appointed commander and killed by midday. Duryodhana's army — eleven akṣauhiṇīs, hundreds of kings, thousands of elephants and chariots — is destroyed in a single day. By afternoon, the king who commanded the largest army ever assembled in the epic is alone, on foot, carrying a mace, walking toward a lake.

The viṣāda here is not anticipatory like Arjuna's, not retrospective like Dhṛtarāṣṭra's. It is the grief of the endgame — when the outcome is determined and the only question remaining is the manner of the ending. Duryodhana knows he has lost. His brothers are dead. His army is gone. What follows is not a battle for victory but a prolonged negotiation over the terms of his death.

---

### The Last King Standing

The opening of the Śalyaparva is Dhṛtarāṣṭra's lament for Karṇa, already killed in the previous parva. Saṃjaya arrives in Hastināpura — wretched, overcome:

> *praviveśa purīṃ dīno duḥkhaśokasamanvitaḥ* (09.001.014)

> He entered the city, wretched, overcome with grief and sorrow.

The word *dīna* — wretched, broken, abject — will attach itself to nearly everyone in this parva. Saṃjaya is *dīna*. Dhṛtarāṣṭra, hearing the full catalogue of the slain, falls to the ground senseless (*nipapāta mahārāja gatasattvo mahītale*, 09.001.037). Vidura falls beside him. Gāndhārī falls. The entire court collapses in a wave of synchronized grief — *niḥsaṃjñaṃ patitaṃ bhūmau tadāsīd rājamaṇḍalam* (09.001.041), "the whole assembly lay unconscious on the ground."

Then Dhṛtarāṣṭra recovers and speaks. His lament is the key verse:

> *aho bata mahad duḥkhaṃ yad ahaṃ pāṇḍavān raṇe*
> *kṣemiṇaś cāvyayāṃś caiva tvattaḥ sūta śṛṇomi vai* (09.002.003)

> What great suffering — that I should hear from you, charioteer, that the Pāṇḍavas are safe and undiminished in battle!

This is not grief for his own sons. It is grief that the *other side* survived. The word *avyaya* — "undiminished," "imperishable" — is the same term the Gītā uses for the ātman (BG 2.17). Here it describes a military fact. The Pāṇḍavas are *avyaya* not metaphysically but strategically. They cannot be diminished. Dhṛtarāṣṭra's great suffering is not loss alone but the recognition that loss was always the outcome. The refrain that follows — *kim anyad bhāgadheyataḥ*, "what else but fate?" — repeated eleven times across verses 09.002.030-039, is not acceptance. It is the cry of a man who has understood too late that agency was never his.

### The Lake

After Śalya falls at noon, Duryodhana is left alone on the battlefield. The text marks this with devastating precision:

> *ekākī bharataśreṣṭha tato duryodhano nṛpaḥ*
> *nāpaśyat samare kaṃ cit sahāyaṃ rathināṃ varaḥ* (09.028.023)

> Alone, O best of Bharatas — Duryodhana the king saw no ally on the field, that best of chariot-warriors.

He abandons his chariot, takes his mace on foot, and walks to Dvaipāyana Lake. On the way, he remembers Vidura's warning — Vidura who foresaw the catastrophe and was ignored:

> *idaṃ nūnaṃ mahāprājño viduro dṛṣṭavān purā*
> *mahad vaiśasam asmākaṃ kṣatriyāṇāṃ ca saṃyuge* (09.028.027)

> Surely the greatly wise Vidura foresaw this long ago — the great slaughter of our side and of the kṣatriyas in battle.

Then Duryodhana enters the lake and solidifies the water by māyā — *astambhayata toyaṃ ca māyayā manujādhipaḥ* (09.028.052). He lies hidden beneath the surface, wounded, cooling his body in the water. He sends a final message through Saṃjaya:

> *suhṛdbhis tādṛśair hīnaḥ putrair bhrātṛbhir eva ca*
> *pāṇḍavaiś ca hṛte rājye ko nu jīvati mādṛśaḥ* (09.028.050)

> Deprived of such friends, of sons and brothers, with my kingdom seized by the Pāṇḍavas — who like me would wish to live?

This is grief spoken from beneath water. The image is extraordinary: a king submerged, speaking his last political statement to a messenger, then sinking into magically solidified water to wait. Not to escape — there is no escape — but to delay the inevitable long enough to choose how it arrives.

---

### The Taunting

When the Pāṇḍavas find the lake, Yudhiṣṭhira's speech is prolonged, cruel, and deliberately calibrated to force Duryodhana out. It strikes at the only thing Duryodhana has left — his identity as a warrior:

> *sa ca darpo naraśreṣṭha sa ca mānaḥ kva te gataḥ*
> *yas tvaṃ saṃstabhya salilaṃ bhīto rājan vyavasthitaḥ* (09.030.018)

> Where has that pride gone, O best of men? Where has that honor gone — you who solidified the water, O king, and stand there in fear?

> *śūramānī na śūras tvaṃ mithyā vadasi bhārata*
> *śūro 'ham iti durbuddhe sarvalokasya śṛṇvataḥ* (09.030.025)

> You think yourself a hero but you are no hero. You speak falsely, O fool, saying "I am brave" — while the whole world listens.

Duryodhana's reply from the water is remarkable in its clarity. He does not deny defeat. He offers the earth itself:

> *yadarthaṃ rājyam icchāmi kurūṇāṃ kurunandana*
> *ta ime nihatāḥ sarve bhrātaro me janeśvara* (09.030.041)

> Those for whose sake I desired the kingdom of the Kurus — those brothers of mine — are all slain, O lord of men.

> *kṣīṇaratnāṃ ca pṛthivīṃ hatakṣatriyapuṃgavām*
> *nābhyutsahāmy ahaṃ bhoktuṃ vidhavām iva yoṣitam* (09.030.042)

> This earth, its treasures spent, its best kṣatriyas slain — I cannot bring myself to enjoy it, like a woman widowed.

The simile is devastating. The earth as widow. The kingdom as a woman whose husband has been killed by the very people now claiming her. Duryodhana will not rule what he has emptied. But Yudhiṣṭhira refuses the gift — *adharmeṇa na gṛhṇīyāṃ tvayā dattāṃ mahīm imām* (09.030.053), "I will not accept this earth given by you through adharma" — and reminds Duryodhana of the needle-point of land he refused before the war: *sūcyagreṇāpi yad bhūmer api dhrīyeta bhārata / tanmātram api no mahyaṃ na dadāti purā bhavān* (09.030.058-059), "not even a needle-point of earth would you give us before."

This exchange is the political equivalent of a philosophical argument. Both sides are correct. Duryodhana is right that the kingdom is worthless now. Yudhiṣṭhira is right that a gift offered after defeat is not a gift. The war has destroyed the object of the war.

### The Mace and the Blow

Duryodhana rises from the lake — *sa bhittvā stambhitaṃ toyaṃ skandhe kṛtvāyasīṃ gadām / udatiṣṭhata putras te pratapan raśmimān iva* (09.031.036), "breaking through the solidified water, placing the iron mace on his shoulder, your son rose like the blazing sun." The epic compares him to Yama with his staff, to Śakra with his thunderbolt, to Hara with his trident. Whatever Duryodhana has done, the text does not diminish him at the end.

Kṛṣṇa, watching, gives the strategic instruction that seals everything:

> *tvam asya sakthinī bhaṅktvā pratijñāṃ pārayiṣyasi* (09.032.024)

> Break his thighs, and you will fulfill your vow.

This is a reference to Bhīma's oath in the sabhā — to shatter Duryodhana's thighs with his mace. The blow, when it comes, is below the waist. Balarāma, who arrives as the impartial teacher of both combatants in mace-fighting, condemns it immediately:

> *aho dhig yad adho nābheḥ prahṛtaṃ śuddhavikrame*
> *naitad dṛṣṭaṃ gadāyuddhe kṛtavān yad vṛkodaraḥ* (09.059.005)

> Shame — that a blow was struck below the navel in fair combat! This has never been seen in mace-fighting — what Vṛkodara has done!

> *adho nābhyā na hantavyam iti śāstrasya niścayaḥ*
> *ayaṃ tv aśāstravin mūḍhaḥ svacchandāt saṃpravartate* (09.059.006)

> "One must not strike below the navel" — this is the settled rule of the śāstra. But this fool, ignorant of the śāstra, acts by his own will.

Kṛṣṇa restrains Balarāma physically — *tam utpatantaṃ jagrāha keśavo vinayānataḥ* (09.059.009) — and argues the case: Bhīma's vow was sworn publicly; Maitreya's curse had foretold this exact injury; the Pāṇḍavas are family. Balarāma is unconvinced but departs, pronouncing his verdict:

> *hatvādharmeṇa rājānaṃ dharmātmānaṃ suyodhanam*
> *jihmayodhīti loke 'smin khyātiṃ yāsyati pāṇḍavaḥ* (09.059.023)

> Having killed the righteous king Suyodhana by adharma, the Pāṇḍava will be known in this world as a crooked fighter.

> *duryodhano 'pi dharmātmā gatiṃ yāsyati śāśvatīm* (09.059.024)

> And Duryodhana, the righteous one, will go to the eternal destination.

Balarāma calls Duryodhana *dharmātman* — "righteous-souled." The same epithet the epic gives to Yudhiṣṭhira. This is not narrative inconsistency. It is the text's refusal to let the moral calculus resolve cleanly.

---

### The Defiance

Duryodhana, broken-thighed, lying in dust, surrounded by jackals and vultures, delivers the most extraordinary speech in the Śalyaparva. The text says he props himself up on his arms, grinding his teeth, shaking his matted hair — *bāhū dharaṇyāṃ niṣpiṣya muhur matta iva dvipaḥ / prakīrṇān mūrdhajān dhunvan dantair dantān upaspṛśan* (09.063.006) — and condemns the Pāṇḍavas' methods. Then he turns from accusation to summation:

> *iṣṭaṃ bhṛtyā bhṛtāḥ samyag bhūḥ praśāstā sasāgarā*
> *mūrdhni sthitam amitrāṇāṃ jīvatām eva saṃjaya* (09.063.018)

> Sacrifices performed, servants properly maintained, the earth ruled to the ocean's edge, my foot placed on the heads of living enemies, O Saṃjaya.

> *svadharmeṇa jitā lokāḥ ko nu svantataro mayā* (09.063.023)

> By my own dharma I have won the worlds — who is more content than I?

> *yad iṣṭaṃ kṣatrabandhūnāṃ svadharmam anutiṣṭhatām*
> *nidhanaṃ tan mayā prāptaṃ ko nu svantataro mayā* (09.063.025)

> The death that is desired by kṣatriyas who follow their own dharma — that death I have obtained. Who is more content than I?

The refrain *ko nu svantataro mayā* — "who is more content than I?" — repeats five times. It is the counterpoint to Dhṛtarāṣṭra's *kim anyad bhāgadheyataḥ* — "what else but fate?" The father's lament and the son's triumph are mirror images. Both ask rhetorical questions. Dhṛtarāṣṭra's questions expect no answer because everything is lost. Duryodhana's questions expect no answer because — in his own framework — everything has been fulfilled.

---

### The Gītā Connection

This is where the Śalyaparva tests the Gītā most severely. BG 2.32 says:

> *yadṛcchayā copapannaṃ svargadvāram apāvṛtam*
> *sukhinaḥ kṣatriyāḥ pārtha labhante yuddham īdṛśam*

> Happy are the kṣatriyas, O Pārtha, who obtain a battle like this — arriving unsought, an open door to heaven.

Duryodhana dies in battle. He does not flee. He fights on foot, alone, against a better-armed enemy, and falls to an illegal blow. By the Gītā's own criteria, he has earned svarga. The Svargārohaṇaparva will confirm this — Duryodhana is in heaven when Yudhiṣṭhira arrives.

But the manner of the fall complicates everything. Bhīma's blow is below the waist. Kṛṣṇa signals for it. Balarāma condemns it. The victors celebrate while jackals circle the broken king. The Gītā teaches svadharma — perform your own duty without attachment to results. Duryodhana claims exactly this: *svadharmeṇa jitā lokāḥ*. He has followed kṣatriya dharma to its end. He has won the worlds.

The question the Śalyaparva poses is not whether svadharma produces a destination. It does. Duryodhana goes to heaven. The question is whether svadharma produces dignity — whether following the warrior code guarantees that the ending will match the code's own aesthetic. The answer the text gives is no. You can follow dharma perfectly and still die with your thigh shattered by a foul blow, your head stepped on by your enemy, jackals waiting to feed. The destination is svarga. The road there passes through dust.

Duryodhana's defiance is genuine. His courage is real. But the viṣāda of endgame is precisely this: that the framework grants him heaven and denies him the clean death that the framework promised. The Gītā says the door to svarga opens in battle. It does not say what happens at the threshold.

> *samantapañcake puṇye triṣu lokeṣu viśrute*
> *ahaṃ nidhanam āsādya lokān prāpsyāmi śāśvatān* (09.063.039)

> In the sacred Samantapañcaka, renowned in the three worlds, having met my death, I shall attain the eternal worlds.

He is right. He does. The viṣāda is that being right was never enough.
