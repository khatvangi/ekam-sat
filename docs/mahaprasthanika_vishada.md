# The Last Walk

## Mahāprasthānika and the Gītā's Aftermath

---

### After Everything

The Mahāprasthānika is the shortest teaching-book of the Mahābhārata — 234 verses in the BORI Critical Edition, three chapters, no philosophical discourses. It contains almost no doctrine. There are no long speeches on the self, no meditation instructions, no cosmic revelations. And yet it is the severest test of the Gītā's claims, because it shows what happened to the people who received the teaching.

The setup is one verse:

> *kālaḥ pacati bhūtāni sarvāṇy eva mahāmate*
> *karmanyāsam ahaṃ manye tvam api draṣṭum arhasi* (17.001.003)

> Time cooks all beings, O wise one.
> I think it is time to lay down our tasks. You too should see this.

Yudhiṣṭhira speaks this to Arjuna — the same Arjuna who received the Gītā. *Kālaḥ pacati bhūtāni* — "Time cooks all beings." In BG 11.32, Kṛṣṇa said *kālo'smi* — "I am Time." Now Time is not a revelation. It is a fact. Kṛṣṇa is dead — killed by Jarā's arrow in the Mausalaparva. The Vṛṣṇis have destroyed each other, as Gāndhārī cursed. Time, which Kṛṣṇa claimed to be, has consumed Kṛṣṇa himself.

Arjuna's response:

> *kālaḥ kāla iti bruvan*
> *anvapadyata tad vākyaṃ bhrātur jyeṣṭhasya vīryavān* (17.001.004)

> Saying "time, time," the valiant one
> assented to the word of his eldest brother.

*Kālaḥ kāla iti bruvan.* "Saying 'time, time.'" The man who received the Gītā's 700 verses, who heard the cosmic revelation, who saw the viśvarūpa — his final philosophical statement is two words, repeated. Time. Time. There is nothing left to understand. The teaching was given. The war was fought. The kingdom was ruled for thirty-six years. Now there is only the walk.

---

### The Departure

They give away the kingdom to Parikṣit, Arjuna's grandson. They put on bark garments. They extinguish the sacrificial fires. The five brothers, Draupadī, and a dog walk out of Hāstinapura.

> *bhrātaraḥ pañca kṛṣṇā ca ṣaṣṭhī śvā caiva saptamaḥ*
> *ātmanā saptamo rājā niryayau gajasāhvayāt* (17.001.023)

> Five brothers, Kṛṣṇā the sixth, and a dog the seventh —
> with himself as the seventh, the king departed from the City of the Elephant.

Seven beings walk east, then south, then west, then north — circumambulating the earth. They pass the ocean. They see Dvārakā drowned by the sea. They reach the Himālayas.

On the way, Agni appears and asks Arjuna to give back the Gāṇḍīva:

> *gāṇḍīvaṃ kārmukaśreṣṭhaṃ varuṇāyaiva dīyatām* (17.001.039)

> Let the Gāṇḍīva, best of bows, be returned to Varuṇa.

The bow that Arjuna dropped in grief in BG 1.47 (*visṛjya saśaraṃ cāpaṃ*), the bow that Kṛṣṇa's teaching made him pick up again — he now throws it into the water. This time not in despair, but in renunciation. The Gītā freed him to fight with it. Time has made it irrelevant. He lets it go into the sea.

---

### They Fall

Then, one by one, they fall. The text gives each death one verse of explanation from Yudhiṣṭhira. No philosophy. No consolation. A diagnosis.

Draupadī falls first:

> *pakṣapāto mahān asyā viśeṣeṇa dhanaṃjaye*
> *tasyaitat phalam adyaiṣā bhuṅkte puruṣasattama* (17.002.006)

> Great was her partiality, especially toward Dhanaṃjaya.
> Today she eats the fruit of that.

Sahadeva:

> *ātmanaḥ sadṛśaṃ prājñaṃ naiṣo 'manyata kaṃ cana*
> *tena doṣeṇa patitas tasmād eṣa nṛpātmajaḥ* (17.002.010)

> He did not consider anyone as wise as himself.
> By that fault this prince has fallen.

Nakula:

> *rūpeṇa matsamo nāsti kaś cid ity asya darśanam*
> *adhikaś cāham evaika ity asya manasi sthitam* (17.002.016)

> "No one equals me in beauty" — that was his view.
> "I alone am superior" — that was fixed in his mind.

Arjuna:

> *ekāhnā nirdaheyaṃ vai śatrūn ity arjuno 'bravīt*
> *na ca tat kṛtavān eṣa śūramānī tato 'patat* (17.002.021)

> "I could burn up the enemies in a single day," Arjuna had said.
> He did not do it. Boasting of his prowess, he fell.

> *avamene dhanurgrāhān eṣa sarvāṃś ca phalgunaḥ*
> *yathā coktaṃ tathā caiva kartavyaṃ bhūtim icchatā* (17.002.022)

> This Phalguṇa despised all other bowmen.
> One who desires well-being should do what one says.

This is the man to whom Kṛṣṇa said *yogasthaḥ kuru karmāṇi* (BG 2.48) — "established in yoga, perform actions." Arjuna performed actions. He also boasted about them. The Gītā's teaching on detachment (*saṅgaṃ tyaktvā*) was received, enacted in battle, and then slowly eroded over thirty-six years of kingship by the residue of pride. The teaching worked. The human being was not permanently transformed by it.

Then Bhīma:

> *bho bho rājan avekṣasva patito 'haṃ priyas tava*
> *kiṃnimittaṃ ca patanaṃ brūhi me yadi vettha ha* (17.002.024)

> "Look, O king! I have fallen — I who am dear to you.
> Tell me the cause of my falling, if you know it."

Yudhiṣṭhira, walking on, does not stop:

> *atyarthaṃ bhavatā bhukto bhakṣāś cokto 'si paraiḥ prabho*
> *anavadhyāyamānaḥ prāṇān atha bhuṅkṣe vṛkodara* (17.002.025a, paraphrased)

The diagnosis: you ate too much, and you did not regard others while you ate.

Yudhiṣṭhira does not stop. He does not grieve. He does not philosophize. He names the fault, he keeps walking, he does not look back. Five people and a dog left Hāstinapura. Now it is one man and a dog.

The Gītā's Kṛṣṇa told Arjuna that the self is undying (BG 2.20). He was right. The self does not die. But the bodies of every person who heard the teaching are now lying on a mountainside, killed not by enemies but by the particular moral flaws that the teaching could name but could not cure.

---

### The Dog

Indra arrives in his chariot:

> *ārohety abravīc ca tam* (17.003.001c)

> "Ascend," he said.

Yudhiṣṭhira asks first about his brothers:

> *bhrātaraḥ patitā me 'tra āgaccheyur mayā saha*
> *na vinā bhrātṛbhiḥ svargam icche gantuṃ sureśvara* (17.003.003)

> My brothers have fallen here. Let them come with me.
> I do not wish to enter heaven without my brothers, O lord of the gods.

Indra says: they have gone ahead. You will see them. Then:

> *nikṣipya mānuṣaṃ dehaṃ gatās te bharatarṣabha*
> *anena tvaṃ śarīreṇa svargaṃ gantā na saṃśayaḥ* (17.003.006)

> Casting off the human body, they have gone, O bull of the Bhāratas.
> You — with this very body — shall go to heaven. There is no doubt.

*Anena tvaṃ śarīreṇa.* "With this very body." The Gītā taught the body-self distinction: the self abandons bodies like garments (BG 2.22). Yudhiṣṭhira is told he does not need to abandon his. He alone goes in the body. The Gītā's teaching on transcendence is enacted literally — but only for the one man who never needed to be taught.

Then the dog:

> *ayaṃ śvā bhūtabhavyeśa bhakto māṃ nityam eva ha*
> *sa gaccheta mayā sārdham ānṛśaṃsyā hi me matiḥ* (17.003.007)

> This dog, O lord of past and future, has been devoted to me always.
> Let him go with me. For compassion is my resolve.

Indra pushes back three times. The argument escalates:

> *amartyatvaṃ matsamatvaṃ ca rājan*
> *śriyaṃ kṛtsnāṃ mahatīṃ caiva kīrtim*
> *saṃprāpto 'dya svargasukhāni ca tvaṃ*
> *tyaja śvānaṃ nātra nṛśaṃsam asti* (17.003.008)

> Immortality, equality with me, O king,
> complete glory, great fame,
> and the pleasures of heaven — you have attained all these today.
> Abandon the dog. There is no cruelty in this.

The offer: everything the Gītā promises — immortality, freedom from suffering, the highest state — in exchange for abandoning one devoted creature.

Yudhiṣṭhira refuses:

> *bhaktatyāgaṃ prāhur atyantapāpaṃ*
> *tulyaṃ loke brahmavadhyākṛtena*
> *tasmān nāhaṃ jātu kathaṃ canādya*
> *tyakṣyāmy enaṃ svasukhārthī mahendra* (17.003.011)

> Abandoning one who is devoted, they say, is the ultimate sin —
> equal in the world to killing a brāhmaṇa.
> Therefore I will never, under any circumstance,
> abandon this one for the sake of my own happiness, O Mahendra.

*Svasukhārthī.* "For the sake of my own happiness." The Gītā's final teaching is *sarvadharmān parityajya mām ekaṃ śaraṇaṃ vraja* (BG 18.66) — "abandon all dharmas, take refuge in me alone." Indra asks the same structure: abandon this attachment, enter the highest state. Yudhiṣṭhira inverts it: I will not abandon the devoted for the sake of the highest state. His dharma is *ānṛśaṃsya* — non-cruelty, compassion — and he will not renounce it for heaven.

Indra pushes once more. Yudhiṣṭhira holds:

> *pratipradānaṃ śaraṇāgatasya*
> *striyā vadho brāhmaṇasvāpahāraḥ*
> *mitradrohas tāni catvāri śakra*
> *bhaktatyāgaś caiva samo mato me* (17.003.015)

> Betraying one who has taken refuge, killing a woman,
> stealing from a brāhmaṇa, treachery against a friend —
> these four, O Śakra, and the abandonment of the devoted —
> these I consider equal.

Five sins, placed as equals. The fifth — *bhaktatyāga*, abandoning the devoted — is his own addition. It is not in any dharmaśāstra list. Yudhiṣṭhira is making law on the spot, standing at the gate of heaven, choosing a dog over immortality.

The dog reveals himself:

> *dharmasvarūpī bhagavān uvāca*
> *yudhiṣṭhiraṃ prītiyukto narendraṃ*
> *ślakṣṇair vākyaiḥ saṃstavasaṃprayuktaiḥ* (17.003.016)

> The blessed one, Dharma in embodied form, spoke
> to Yudhiṣṭhira the king, filled with joy,
> in gentle words joined with praise.

The dog was Dharma. Yudhiṣṭhira's father. Testing him for the last time, as he tested him once before at the lake in the Āraṇyakaparva. The test was not whether Yudhiṣṭhira understood the metaphysics. It was whether he would sacrifice compassion for transcendence.

### What the Gītā Cannot Contain

The Mahāprasthānika tests the Gītā's promises against their terminal consequences.

The Gītā teaches: the self is undying (BG 2.20). The Mahāprasthānika shows: the bodies of everyone who heard this teaching lie on a mountainside, each killed by a moral flaw the teaching could identify but not cure.

The Gītā teaches: act without attachment to results (BG 2.47). The Mahāprasthānika shows: Arjuna acted — and the residue of pride accumulated over thirty-six years, invisible during the war, fatal on the mountain.

The Gītā teaches: abandon all dharmas, take refuge in me alone (BG 18.66). The Mahāprasthānika shows: when heaven itself offers this deal — abandon the devoted, enter the highest state — the right answer is no. Yudhiṣṭhira's ānṛśaṃsya is not a dharma the Gītā teaches. It is not one of the 123 nodes. It is not naiṣkāmya karma, not sthitaprajñā, not guṇātīta, not sarvadharmān parityajya. It is something simpler: do not abandon the creature that followed you.

The pipeline finds 51 echoes in the Mahāprasthānika. What it cannot find is the absence. 102 of 123 BG nodes are not present here. The Mahāprasthānika does not teach the guṇas, does not discuss prakṛti, does not analyze action and inaction, does not distinguish sāṃkhya from yoga. It does one thing: it watches people die and tests whether the survivor will betray compassion for the sake of liberation.

The answer the Mahābhārata gives — through the mouth of Yudhiṣṭhira, in the last scene before heaven — is that compassion is not one dharma among many. It is the one that cannot be traded away.

*Bhaktatyāgaṃ prāhur atyantapāpam.*

Abandoning the devoted is the ultimate sin.

Not even the Gītā overrides this.
