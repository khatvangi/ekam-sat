# The Viṣāda of the Dying Teacher

## Bhīṣma's Arrow-Bed and the Knowledge He Could Not Use

---

### The Situation

The Anuśāsanaparva — "The Book of the Teaching" — is the thirteenth of the Mahābhārata's eighteen books, and it opens with a man who cannot stop talking because he is about to die. Bhīṣma lies on his bed of arrows on the field of Kurukṣetra, his body pierced by the shafts that hold him above the ground, teaching dharma to Yudhiṣṭhira. He has been doing this since the Śāntiparva, the previous book. The Anuśāsana continues where the Śānti left off, but the register shifts. Where the Śāntiparva addressed rājadharma and mokṣadharma — the duties of kings, the path to liberation — the Anuśāsana turns to the practical business of living: giving, conduct, ancestor rites, the dharma of women, the dharma of householders. These are the things a dying man teaches when he has finished with theory and wants to make sure the survivor knows how to actually behave.

The opening verse establishes the scene's emotional truth. It is not Bhīṣma who grieves. It is Yudhiṣṭhira:

> *śarācitaśarīraṃ hi tīvravraṇam udīkṣya ca*
> *śamaṃ nopalabhe vīra duṣkṛtāny eva cintayan* (13.001.003)

> Seeing your body covered with arrows, your wounds severe,
> I find no peace, O hero — I can only think of my own sins.

And then the image that haunts the whole book:

> *rudhireṇāvasiktāṅgaṃ prasravantaṃ yathācalam*
> *tvāṃ dṛṣṭvā puruṣavyāghra sīde varṣāsv ivāmbujam* (13.001.004)

> Seeing you drenched in blood, streaming like a mountain,
> O tiger among men — I wilt like a lotus in the rains.

*Sīde.* "I wilt," "I sink." The verb is the same family as *sīdanti mama gātrāṇi* — "my limbs sink down" — from Arjuna's collapse in BG 1.29. The echo is precise. Arjuna sank before the war because he could see what it would cost. Yudhiṣṭhira sinks after the war because he can see what it cost, embodied in the bleeding body of the man who raised him.

Yudhiṣṭhira names his position directly:

> *ahaṃ tava hy antakaraḥ suhṛdvadhakaras tathā*
> *na śāntim adhigacchāmi paśyaṃs tvāṃ duḥkhitaṃ kṣitau* (13.001.007)

> I am the one who brought your end, the killer of friends.
> I find no peace, seeing you suffering on the ground.

*Antakaraḥ.* "The one who causes the end." Not the killer — that was Arjuna's arrow, guided by Śikhaṇḍin. But the one responsible. Yudhiṣṭhira claims the agency the Gītā tried to dissolve. Kṛṣṇa told Arjuna: these men are already slain by Time, you are merely the instrument (*nimittamātraṃ bhava*, BG 11.33). Yudhiṣṭhira, twelve books later, does not use that argument. He says *aham* — I.

This is the viṣāda of the Anuśāsana. Not the teacher's grief but the student's: receiving wisdom from a man whose body is the direct evidence of what the student's war has done.

---

### What Bhīṣma Teaches

Bhīṣma's response to Yudhiṣṭhira's guilt is not consolation. It is instruction. He answers the grief by burying it under content. Over 154 chapters — some six thousand verses — he delivers what amounts to a complete curriculum of dharma. The subjects include:

**Dāna** — the dharma of giving. Who to give to, when, how much, in what spirit. Not metaphysics but logistics:

> *dānaṃ hi mahatī kriyā* (13.009.022c)

> Giving is a great act.

> *tasmād dātavyam eveha pratiśrutya yudhiṣṭhira*
> *yadīcchec chobhanāṃ jātiṃ prāptuṃ bharatasattama* (13.009.021)

> Therefore one must give, having promised, O Yudhiṣṭhira,
> if one desires to attain a good birth, O best of the Bharatas.

**Ācāra** — conduct, behavior, the daily practices that constitute a dharmic life. The Anuśāsana's teaching on ācāra is a single, devastating sentence:

> *ācāralakṣaṇo dharmaḥ santaś cācāralakṣaṇāḥ*
> *sādhūnāṃ ca yathā vṛttam etad ācāralakṣaṇam* (13.107.009)

> Dharma is marked by conduct. The good are marked by conduct.
> The way the virtuous live — that is the mark of conduct.

The circularity is deliberate. Dharma is what the good do. The good are those who do dharma. There is no external standard, no metaphysical anchor — only the living example. This is not the Gītā's framework, where dharma is grounded in the nature of the self and the structure of reality. This is practical wisdom: watch the good, do what they do.

**Śrāddha** — ancestor rites, the obligations to the dead. When to perform them, what to offer, which Brāhmaṇas to invite, which to exclude:

> *śrāddhasya brāhmaṇaḥ kālaḥ prāptaṃ dadhi ghṛtaṃ tathā* (13.024.035a)

> The right time for the śrāddha, the right Brāhmaṇa, the right curds and ghee.

And then, embedded within this curriculum of earthly dharma, the text's most famous passage: the **Viṣṇusahasranāma**, the thousand names of Viṣṇu. Bhīṣma introduces it with full awareness of what he is doing:

> *viṣṇor nāmasahasraṃ me śṛṇu pāpabhayāpaham* (13.135.012c)

> Hear from me the thousand names of Viṣṇu, which destroy the fear of sin.

And the litany begins:

> *viśvaṃ viṣṇur vaṣaṭkāro bhūtabhavyabhavatprabhuḥ*
> *bhūtakṛd bhūtabhṛd bhāvo bhūtātmā bhūtabhāvanaḥ* (13.135.014)

> The universe, Viṣṇu, the sacrificial cry, lord of past, future, and present,
> maker of beings, sustainer of beings, existence itself, the self of beings, the cause of beings.

A dying warrior-sage, his body suspended on arrows, reciting the names of God. The juxtaposition is the point. The Gītā's theophany — Kṛṣṇa revealing his cosmic form in chapter 11 — comes from the divine downward to the human. The Viṣṇusahasranāma goes in the opposite direction: a human being, pierced and bleeding, reaching upward through language to enumerate divinity, name by name, before his breath runs out.

### The Paradox Bhīṣma Cannot Resolve

The Anuśāsana's viṣāda is not only the student's. There is a deeper grief that the text never names directly but that the Mahābhārata's audience cannot miss: Bhīṣma knew all of this before the war.

He knew dharma. He is the Mahābhārata's supreme authority on it — the man to whom both the Śāntiparva and the Anuśāsana are assigned, the teacher whose deathbed curriculum fills nearly twenty thousand verses across two books. And yet, in the Sabhāparva, when Draupadī was dragged into the assembly hall and asked whether a man who has already gambled himself away can stake his wife, Bhīṣma — the man now teaching ācāra, dāna, dharma of kings, dharma of women — sat and said this:

> *na vivektuṃ ca te praśnam etaṃ śaknomi niścayāt*
> *sūkṣmatvād gahanatvāc ca kāryasyāsya ca gauravāt* (02.062.016)

> I cannot resolve your question with certainty,
> because of the subtlety, the impenetrability, and the gravity of this matter.

*Sūkṣmatvāt.* "Because of its subtlety." The same word he uses in the Anuśāsana — *sūkṣma* — to describe the fine points of dharma that he then proceeds to expound for six thousand verses. In the Sabhā, subtlety is his excuse for silence. On the arrow-bed, subtlety is his subject.

He saw it clearly enough:

> *nūnam antaḥ kulasyāsya bhavitā nacirād iva*
> *tathā hi kuravaḥ sarve lobhamohaparāyaṇāḥ* (02.062.017)

> Surely the end of this family will come before long,
> for all the Kurus are driven by greed and delusion.

He predicted the catastrophe. He diagnosed its cause — *lobhamohaparāyaṇāḥ*, "devoted to greed and delusion," the same two afflictions the Gītā maps in chapters 2 and 3. He saw the future. He knew the dharma. And he sat in silence while Duḥśāsana pulled at Draupadī's garment.

This is the wound the Anuśāsana cannot heal. The text never stages a confrontation between Bhīṣma's knowledge and his failure to act on it. It simply places the teaching after the catastrophe and lets the reader hold both facts simultaneously: this man knows everything about dharma, and this man did nothing when dharma required action.

---

### Two Teachers, Two Registers

The Gītā is taught by the divine to a warrior before battle. The Anuśāsana is taught by a dying warrior-sage to a king after battle. The structures are inversions of each other.

Kṛṣṇa teaches from certainty. He knows the nature of the self, the structure of action, the identity of all beings with Brahman. His knowledge is not acquired — it is his nature. He does not teach because time is running out. He teaches because Arjuna has collapsed and needs to be restored to function.

Bhīṣma teaches from urgency. He counts the days:

> *aṣṭapañcāśataṃ rātryaḥ śayānasyādya me gatāḥ*
> *śareṣu niśitāgreṣu yathā varṣaśataṃ tathā* (13.153.027)

> Fifty-eight nights have now passed as I lie here
> on arrows with sharpened tips — each night like a hundred years.

*Yathā varṣaśataṃ tathā.* "Like a hundred years." The man who chose the boon of dying at his own time — *icchāmṛtyu*, death at will — has been lying on sharpened arrows for fifty-eight nights, and each night feels like a century. He has been waiting for the sun to turn northward, for the auspicious moment. The teaching fills the waiting. Every discourse on dāna, every catalogue of conduct, every thousand-name hymn is spoken into the narrowing gap between now and the moment he will release his breath.

The Gītā's knowledge liberates. It frees Arjuna from paralysis, restores him to action, and the action — the war — unfolds. Bhīṣma's knowledge is being given away. Its owner can no longer use it. He is transferring a lifetime's accumulation of dharma to the one person who must now govern, because Bhīṣma himself is leaving. The teaching is an inheritance, not a liberation.

And the content itself diverges. The Gītā subordinates all dharma to jñāna and bhakti — knowledge of the self and devotion to the divine. Arjuna is told that action performed without attachment is the path, and that all paths converge on surrender to Kṛṣṇa. The Anuśāsana teaches the vast body of dharma that the Gītā compresses or ignores: how much to give and to whom, how to perform ancestor rites correctly, what conduct constitutes a virtuous life, how a householder should behave in the morning, what a king owes to Brāhmaṇas. This is the MBH-extra — the enormous social, ritual, and ethical teaching that surrounds the Gītā's metaphysics like the earth surrounds a seed.

### The Departure

When Bhīṣma finally releases his life, the text records it with yogic precision:

> *dhārayām āsa cātmānaṃ dhāraṇāsu yathākramam*
> *tasyordhvam agaman prāṇāḥ saṃniruddhā mahātmanaḥ* (13.154.002)

> He held his self steady through the stages of concentration.
> His vital breaths, restrained, moved upward.

> *saṃniruddhas tu tenātmā sarveṣv āyataneṣu vai*
> *jagāma bhittvā mūrdhānaṃ divam abhyutpapāta ca* (13.154.005)

> His self, withdrawn from all the seats of sensation,
> broke through the crown of his head and flew upward into the sky.

> *maholkeva ca bhīṣmasya mūrdhadeśāj janādhipa*
> *niḥsṛtyākāśam āviśya kṣaṇenāntaradhīyata* (13.154.006)

> Like a great meteor from Bhīṣma's head, O lord of men,
> it burst forth, entered the sky, and in an instant disappeared.

*Maholkeva.* "Like a great meteor." The prāṇa that sustained fifty-eight nights of teaching on sharpened arrows leaves the body through the brahmarandhra — the crown aperture — and vanishes into space. The Gītā promised this: *ūrdhvaṃ gacchanti sattvasthā*, "those established in sattva go upward" (BG 14.18). Bhīṣma, who knew the Gītā's teaching because he was alive when Kṛṣṇa spoke it, achieves the upward departure that the Gītā describes. The knowledge works. It liberates the knower.

But it did not prevent the dice game. It did not stop the war. It did not save Draupadī in the assembly hall.

Kṛṣṇa, after Bhīṣma's departure, consoles the river Gaṅgā, who rises from the water weeping for her son:

> *svacchandena sutas tubhyaṃ gataḥ svargaṃ śubhānane*
> *na śaktāḥ syur nihantuṃ hi raṇe taṃ sarvadevatāḥ* (13.154.031)

> Your son went to heaven of his own will, O fair-faced one.
> Not all the gods together could have killed him in battle.

*Svacchandena.* "Of his own will." Death at will — the boon he earned through his original sacrifice, when he renounced marriage and kingship so his father could wed Satyavatī. The same will that allowed him to choose when to die is the will that did not choose to act when Draupadī asked her question in the Sabhā. Bhīṣma's freedom was always selective. He could master death but not the politics of the court he served.

The Anuśāsana's viṣāda, then, is the grief of knowing that knowledge is not enough. Bhīṣma knew more dharma than any other character in the Mahābhārata. He taught it to Yudhiṣṭhira across two massive books while dying on a bed of arrows. His final departure was yogically perfect. And his life contained the single greatest failure of dharmic action the epic records: silence in the face of injustice, by the man who knew better than anyone what justice required.

The Gītā says knowledge liberates. The Anuśāsana says: yes — but only the knower. The world the knower failed to protect does not benefit from his liberation. It inherits his teaching, delivered too late, from a body that is already leaving.
