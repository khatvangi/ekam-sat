# The Curse Fulfilled

## Mausala and the Death of Kṛṣṇa

---

### The Thirty-Sixth Year

The Mausalaparva opens with the number Gāndhārī named. In the Strīparva, standing over her dead sons, she cursed Kṛṣṇa:

> *tvam apy upasthite varṣe ṣaṭtriṃśe madhusūdana*
> *hatajñātir hatāmātyo hataputro vanecara*
> *kutsitena abhyupāyena nidhanaṃ samavāpsyasi* (11.025.041)

> In the thirty-sixth year, O Madhusūdana —
> your kinsmen slain, your counselors slain, your sons slain, a wanderer in the forest —
> by a lowly means you shall meet your end.

The Mausalaparva begins:

> *ṣaṭtriṃśe tv atha saṃprāpte varṣe kauravanandanaḥ*
> *dadarśa viparītāni nimittāni yudhiṣṭhiraḥ* (16.001.001)

> When the thirty-sixth year arrived, the scion of the Kurus
> saw omens reversed.

*Ṣaṭtriṃśe.* The same number. The curse is a clock. Kṛṣṇa knew. He said so in the Strīparva: *saṃhartā vṛṣṇicakrasya nānyo mad vidyate* (11.025.044) — "no destroyer of the Vṛṣṇi circle other than me." He accepted the thirty-sixth year as his horizon. Now it arrives.

---

### The Iron Club

The origin of the destruction is a prank. Young Yādava warriors dress Sāmba as a pregnant woman and bring him to visiting sages — Viśvāmitra, Kaṇva, Nārada — and ask, mockingly: what will she bear?

The sages, insulted, answer with a curse:

> *vṛṣṇyandhakavināśāya musalaṃ ghoram āyasam*
> *vāsudevasya dāyādaḥ sāmbo 'yaṃ janayiṣyati* (16.002.008)

> For the destruction of the Vṛṣṇis and Andhakas,
> a terrible iron club —
> Sāmba, this descendant of Vāsudeva, shall give birth to it.

> *jarā kṛṣṇaṃ mahātmānaṃ śayānaṃ bhuvi bhetsyati* (16.002.010c)

> And old age — Jarā — shall strike Kṛṣṇa, the great-souled, as he lies on the ground.

Two curses in two verses. The Vṛṣṇis will destroy themselves with a club born from a mockery. Kṛṣṇa will be struck by a hunter named Jarā — whose name means "old age," "decay." The divine who declared *kālo'smi* — "I am Time" — will be consumed by Time's own agent.

Kṛṣṇa hears the curse. His response is one verse:

> *athābravīt tadā vṛṣṇīñ śrutvaivaṃ madhusūdanaḥ*
> *antajño matimāṃs tasya bhavitavyaṃ tatheti tān* (16.002.013)

> Then Madhusūdana, having heard, said to the Vṛṣṇis —
> he who knows the end of things, the wise one:
> "It will be so."

*Bhavitavyaṃ tathā.* "It will be as it must be." Three words. No teaching. No correction. No eighteen chapters. The divine who gave 700 verses of instruction to one warrior's grief responds to the doom of his own clan with three words of acceptance.

Then:

> *kṛtāntam anyathā naicchad kartuṃ sa jagataḥ prabhuḥ* (16.002.014c)

> The lord of the world did not wish to make the decreed end otherwise.

*Kṛtāntam anyathā naicchad kartuṃ.* He did not wish to alter what was decreed. The Gītā's Kṛṣṇa is the one who decrees: *kālo'smi*, I am Time, the great destroyer. The Mausalaparva's Kṛṣṇa does not resist what Time decrees — even when it is aimed at him. He does not alter karma. He inhabits it.

### The Brawl

They go to the seashore at Prabhāsa for a pilgrimage — a pilgrimage Kṛṣṇa himself ordered, knowing what will happen:

> *evaṃ paśyan hṛṣīkeśaḥ saṃprāptaṃ kālaparyayam... prābravīd idam* (16.003.016-017a)

> Seeing the turning of time had come, Hṛṣīkeśa spoke.

He sees it coming. He sends them to the shore. They drink. They quarrel. Sātyaki taunts Kṛtavarmā over the night massacre — the same Sauptikaparva atrocity:

> *kaḥ kṣatriyo manyamānaḥ suptān hanyān mṛtān iva*
> *na tan mṛṣyanti hārdikya yādavā yat tvayā kṛtam* (16.004.017)

> What kṣatriya who calls himself a warrior would kill the sleeping as though already dead?
> The Yādavas do not forgive what you have done, O Hārdikya.

Kṛpa's warning from the Sauptikaparva — *na vadhaḥ pūjyate loke suptānām iha dharmataḥ* (10.005.009), "killing the sleeping is not honored by dharma" — echoes here across five books. The moral debt of the night massacre is still active. It detonates among the Yādavas.

Sātyaki draws his sword and beheads Kṛtavarmā — in Kṛṣṇa's presence:

> *tathānyān api nighnantaṃ yuyudhānaṃ samantataḥ*
> *abhyadhāvad dhṛṣīkeśo vinivārayiṣus tadā* (16.004.028-029)

> As Yuyudhāna struck others all around,
> Hṛṣīkeśa rushed forward to restrain him.

He tries to stop it. He fails. Pradyumna, Kṛṣṇa's own son, rushes in to save Sātyaki:

> *sa bhojaiḥ saha saṃyuktaḥ sātyakiś cāndhakaiḥ saha*
> *bahutvān nihatau tatra ubhau kṛṣṇasya paśyataḥ* (16.004.033)

> Overwhelmed by numbers — Sātyaki by the Bhojas, Pradyumna by the Andhakas —
> both were killed there, before Kṛṣṇa's eyes.

*Kṛṣṇasya paśyataḥ.* "While Kṛṣṇa watched." The same phrase Gāndhārī used in her curse: *upekṣitā vinaśyantas tvayā* (11.025.036) — "while you looked on, they perished." The structure repeats. At Kurukṣetra he watched the Kurus die. At Prabhāsa he watches his own clan die. The grammar is identical.

Then Kṛṣṇa himself picks up a fistful of reeds — and the reeds become iron clubs:

> *erakāṇāṃ tadā muṣṭiṃ kopāj jagrāha keśavaḥ*
> *tad abhūn musalaṃ ghoraṃ vajrakalpam ayomayam*
> *jaghāna tena kṛṣṇas tān ye 'sya pramukhato 'bhavan* (16.004.034-035)

> In rage, Keśava seized a fistful of reeds.
> It became a terrible club, hard as a thunderbolt, made of iron.
> Kṛṣṇa slew with it those who stood before him.

The teacher of *naiṣkāmya karma* — "act without desire for the fruit" — kills his own clansmen with a club. Not in cosmic revelation. Not in the viśvarūpa. In a drunken brawl on a beach, with a fistful of grass that turns to iron.

> *avadhīt pitaraṃ putraḥ pitā putraṃ ca bhārata*
> *mattāḥ paripatanti sma pothayantaḥ parasparam* (16.004.040)

> Sons killed fathers. Fathers killed sons, O Bhārata.
> Drunk, they fell upon each other, crushing one another.

*Parasparakṛtaṃ nāśam* — "destruction wrought by one another" — the exact phrase Kṛṣṇa used when accepting Gāndhārī's curse (11.025.045). He named this. He foresaw it. He did not prevent it. And now he participates in it.

### Kṛṣṇa's Death

Balarāma dies first. Kṛṣṇa sees a great serpent leave his brother's body — Ananta-Śeṣa returning to the ocean. Then Kṛṣṇa goes alone into the forest:

> *tato gate bhrātari vāsudevo*
> *jānan sarvā gatayo divyadṛṣṭiḥ*
> *vane śūnye vicaraṃś cintayāno*
> *bhūmau tataḥ saṃviveśāgryatejāḥ* (16.005.016)

> Then, his brother gone, Vāsudeva —
> who knows all destinies with his divine sight —
> wandering in the empty forest, reflecting,
> lay down upon the ground, he of supreme radiance.

> *sarvaṃ hi tena prāk tadā vittam āsīd*
> *gāndhāryā yad vākyam uktaḥ sa pūrvam* (16.005.017a-b)

> For he had known all this beforehand —
> what Gāndhārī had spoken to him before.

*Sarvaṃ hi tena prāk tadā vittam āsīt.* "He had known all this beforehand." The narrator confirms: Kṛṣṇa knew from the moment of Gāndhārī's curse. He knew when he accepted it with a faint smile. He knew when he ordered the pilgrimage. He knew when he picked up the reeds. He knew when he lay down in the forest. Omniscience does not exempt from karma. It only means you see it coming.

Then Jarā — the hunter whose name means "old age":

> *sa keśavaṃ yogayuktaṃ śayānaṃ*
> *mṛgāśaṅkī lubdhakaḥ sāyakena*
> *jarāvidhyat pādatale tvarāvāṃs*
> *taṃ cābhitas tajjighṛkṣur jagāma* (16.005.020)

> Keśava, absorbed in yoga, lying there —
> the hunter Jarā, thinking him a deer,
> quickly struck him in the sole of his foot with an arrow,
> and then approached to seize his prey.

> *athāpaśyat puruṣaṃ yogayuktaṃ*
> *pītāmbaraṃ lubdhako 'nekabāhum* (16.005.020e)

> Then the hunter saw: a man in yoga,
> wearing yellow silk, with many arms.

> *matvātmānam aparāddhaṃ sa tasya*
> *jagrāha pādau śirasā cārtarūpaḥ* (16.005.021a-b)

> Knowing he had committed an offense,
> the hunter grasped his feet, touching his head to them in anguish.

> *āśvāsayat taṃ mahātmā tadānīṃ*
> *gacchann ūrdhvaṃ rodasī vyāpya lakṣmyā* (16.005.021c-d)

> The great-souled one consoled him then,
> even as he rose upward, filling the two worlds with splendor.

Kṛṣṇa consoles his own killer. His last act on earth is not a teaching, not a revelation, not a command. It is compassion toward a man who shot him by accident. *Āśvāsayat* — he reassured, he comforted. The word used for consolation, not instruction.

Then he rises:

> *tato rājan bhagavān ugratejā*
> *nārāyaṇaḥ prabhavaś cāvyayaś ca*
> *yogācāryo rodasī vyāpya lakṣmyā*
> *sthānaṃ prāpa svaṃ mahātmāprameyam* (16.005.023)

> Then, O king, the blessed one of fierce radiance —
> Nārāyaṇa, the origin and the imperishable —
> the master of yoga, filling both worlds with splendor,
> reached his own place, the great-souled, the immeasurable.

*Avyayaḥ.* "The imperishable." The same word Aśvatthāman used as an epithet of Śiva before the night massacre (10.007.002). The same word the Gītā uses for the ātman (BG 2.17). Here it describes Kṛṣṇa rising from a body killed by an arrow in the foot.

The body-self distinction holds. The self is imperishable. Nārāyaṇa returns to his place. And the body that taught the Gītā, that smiled at Gāndhārī's curse, that picked up the iron reeds and killed, that lay down in the forest and was shot by a hunter who thought he was a deer — that body is dead.

### Arjuna's Failure

The aftermath is Arjuna's. He goes to Dvārakā to evacuate Kṛṣṇa's widows and the surviving Yādavas. On the road, bandits attack the caravan. Arjuna, the greatest archer in the epic, reaches for the Gāṇḍīva:

> *tato 'rjuno dhanur divyaṃ gāṇḍīvam ajaraṃ mahat*
> *āropayitum ārebhe yatnād iva kathaṃ cana* (16.008.052)

> Then Arjuna began to string the divine bow Gāṇḍīva, great and ageless —
> with effort, somehow, barely.

> *cakāra sajyaṃ kṛcchreṇa saṃbhrame tumule sati*
> *cintayām āsa cāstrāṇi na ca sasmāra tāny api* (16.008.053)

> He strung it with difficulty in the tumult of the battle.
> He thought of his divine weapons — and could not remember them.

*Na ca sasmāra tāny api.* "He could not remember them." The astras — the divine weapons Droṇa taught him, the weapons he wielded through eighteen days of war, the weapons the Gītā's teaching freed him to use — are gone. His arms are weak. His bow barely bends. His arrows run out.

> *kṣaṇena tasya te rājan kṣayaṃ jagmur ajihmagāḥ*
> *akṣayā hi purā bhūtvā kṣīṇāḥ kṣatajabhojanāḥ* (16.008.059)

> In a moment, O king, his arrows were exhausted —
> those that were once inexhaustible, now spent, drinkers of blood.

The inexhaustible quivers are empty. The man who could not be defeated in battle watches bandits carry off Kṛṣṇa's widows while he flails with the butt of his bow:

> *dhanuṣkoṭyā tadā dasyūn avadhīt pākaśāsaniḥ* (16.008.060c)

> Then Arjuna struck the bandits with the tip of his bow.

*Dhanuṣkoṭyā.* "With the bow-tip." Not arrows. Not astras. The physical object, used as a club. The greatest archer in Sanskrit literature, reduced to clubbing bandits with the wrong end of his bow while women are abducted around him.

> *prekṣatas tv eva pārthasya vṛṣṇyandhakavarastriyaḥ*
> *jagmur ādāya te mlecchāḥ samantāj janamejaya* (16.008.061)

> Before the very eyes of Pārtha, the noble women of the Vṛṣṇis and Andhakas
> were carried off by the barbarians from all sides, O Janamejaya.

*Prekṣatas tv eva pārthasya.* "Before the very eyes of Pārtha." The phrase again: watching, unable to prevent. Kṛṣṇa watched the Kurus die. Kṛṣṇa watched the Vṛṣṇis die. Now Arjuna watches Kṛṣṇa's own women taken.

The Gītā freed Arjuna to act. Time has revoked the gift. The teaching was not a permanent transformation. It was a capacity granted for a specific purpose at a specific time, and when the time passes, the capacity passes with it.

### What the Gītā Cannot Contain

The Mausalaparva is the fulfillment of everything the Strīparva set in motion. Gāndhārī's curse (11.025.039-041) specified: the thirty-sixth year, the kinsmen slain by each other, the lowly death. Every detail comes true.

But the Mausalaparva is also the fulfillment of the Gītā's own theology — pushed to its terminal consequence. The Gītā teaches:

*Kālo'smi* — "I am Time" (BG 11.32). The Mausala shows: Time consumes the one who claims to be Time. Kṛṣṇa does not escape kāla. He embodies it and is consumed by it.

*Nimitta-mātraṃ bhava* — "be merely the instrument" (BG 11.33). The Mausala shows: Kṛṣṇa himself is the instrument of his clan's destruction, picking up the iron reeds and killing.

*Na hanyate hanyamāne śarīre* — "the self is not killed when the body is killed" (BG 2.20). The Mausala shows: true. Nārāyaṇa rises. But the body that taught this truth lies on the forest floor with an arrow in its foot, shot by a hunter who thought it was a deer.

The pipeline finds 123 echoes in the Mausalaparva. What it cannot find is the structural irony: the same words that liberated Arjuna in the Gītā now describe the destruction of the teacher. *Avyaya* — imperishable — is both the ātman the Gītā teaches and the Kṛṣṇa who rises from a killed body. The word is the same. The situation makes it mean something the Gītā alone could never contain.

The Mausalaparva's deepest claim is not that the teaching fails. The teaching holds. The self IS imperishable. Nārāyaṇa DOES return to his own place. But the teaching does not prevent the brawl, does not stop the iron clubs, does not shield the women from bandits, does not keep the arrows in the quiver. The teaching describes reality. It does not govern events. The self is indestructible — and the world is what it is.

Kṛṣṇa's last earthly act: consoling the hunter who killed him. Not teaching. Consoling. *Āśvāsayat* — he reassured. The divine who gave 700 verses of instruction to Arjuna, and 5 verses of harsh truth to Gāndhārī, and 3 words of acceptance to the sages' curse — his final word to the final person he meets is comfort. Not *aśocyān anvaśocas tvaṃ* — "you grieve for those not worth grieving for." Just: it is all right. You did not know.

*Bhavitavyaṃ tathā.* It will be as it must be.
