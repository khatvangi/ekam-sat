# śrīrāma rāmeti rāme rāme manorame — Corrected Analysis

## What Survives Scrutiny

After critical review, the analysis reduces from 9 "hidden words" to 2 genuine findings,
1 structural observation, and 1 reframing of Śiva's claim.

---

### Finding 1: Pascal's Triangle (mathematical, verified)

29 characters. 13 independent split points. 2^13 = 8,192 valid readings.
Distribution = exact Pascal's Triangle row 13: 1, 13, 78, 286, 715, 1287, 1716, 1716, 1287, 715, 286, 78, 13, 1.

No other verse analyzed (VS, Śiva SN) has this property.
The verse is combinatorially free — every split choice independent of every other.
This is verified mathematics. No interpretation required.

### Finding 2: eti (एति) — a conjugated verb at a word boundary

Position 10-13: e-t-i. Contiguous.
A real Sanskrit finite verb: √i + ti = "he/she/it goes, approaches" (3rd person singular present).

The tradition reads: rāma + iti = "thus: Rāma" (quotation).
The alternative: rām + eti = "Rāma approaches" (verb).

This is genuine. A conjugated verb — not a root fragment, not a stem, but a complete
grammatical form with person/number marking — emerges at a word boundary. The name
contains the verb of the divine's approach. Not stated. Enacted.

### Finding 3: tirā (तिरा) — positional encoding

Position 11-15: t-i-r-ā. Contiguous.
tirā = "across, beyond" from √tṝ (to cross).
Same root as tīrtha (sacred ford), avatāra (descent/crossing-down).

The word for CROSSING sits at the CROSSING POINT between two traditional word boundaries
(rāmeti | rāme → rāme | tirā | me). To see it, you must cross the boundary.

The form enacts the content. This is architecturally interesting regardless of authorial intent.

### Structural observation: √ram recursion

rāma, rame, rāme, manorame — all derive from √ram (to delight, to play, to rest).
The root regenerates across the string. The verse performs delight through phonological recursion.
This is audible in recitation, which matters for a mantra.

Not a "hidden word" — the root is visibly present in the surface reading.
But the recursion — the SAME root appearing in 4+ forms across 29 characters — is the
structural principle that produces the Pascal's Triangle property.

## The 15/15 VS Theme Coverage Table — WITHDRAWN

The table claiming all 15 VS themes are "covered" by the hidden words was unfalsifiable.
Any 22 Sanskrit morphemes would map to 12-15 of those 15 categories because the categories
are designed to be exhaustive (they cover all of reality).

**The control test was not run.** Without showing that a RANDOM 29-character Sanskrit string
fails to achieve 15/15, the finding is trivial.

## The Corrected Argument for Śiva's Claim

Śiva says: *sahasranāma tattulyaṃ rāmanāma varānane* — "the Rāma-name is equal to the thousand names."

**The wrong argument (withdrawn):** The Rāma verse contains all VS themes as substrings.

**The right argument:** The Rāma verse and the VS are two solutions to the same problem:
*how do you demonstrate that the divine cannot be captured in a single designation?*

The VS solves it by **enumeration**: here are 1,000 names, each a different facet.
The Rāma verse solves it by **compression**: here is ONE name that, subjected to
splitting and recombination, generates inexhaustible readings.

The VS is the output. The Rāma verse is the generating function.
*Sahasranāma tattulyam* = **the generator equals its output.**

This is a structural claim about what kind of text each one is.
It does not require finding 15 themes in 22 substrings.
It requires only the mathematically verified fact that one name,
in 29 characters, yields 8,192 readings by splitting alone —
and that this number exceeds what any human lifetime of recitation can exhaust.

Both texts point to the same conclusion: the divine is inexhaustible.
One proves it by listing. The other proves it by generating.
Śiva says they are equal. The mathematics confirms: both are infinite
relative to the human capacity to receive them.
